\begin{abstract}
This project asks whether a decentralized, parameter-shared two-attacker soccer policy trained in
a fast abstract simulator can transfer unchanged to an independently implemented Pymunk
rigid-body simulator under action delays, perception error, communication loss, locomotion
variation, and ball/kick changes. Phase 1 established a corrected, policy-sensitive benchmark.
Phase 2 trained nominal, uniformly randomized, and failure-directed MAPPO for 2,007,040 steps each
and evaluated their frozen best checkpoints. In the single-seed pilot, failure-directed training
raises nominal Pymunk success from 0.440 to 0.640 and the 18-profile mean from 0.417 to 0.521, while
reducing invalid actions and redundant chasing. The result is not uniformly robust: its
action-delay/localization-noise grid score is 0.366 versus 0.494 for nominal MAPPO, with severe
degradation once delay is introduced. Valid passes are too rare to support a learned-cooperation
claim. The result is therefore a non-trivial and promising pilot, not a completed scientific
claim. The immediate goal is targeted delay diagnosis followed by a small, pre-registered
multi-seed confirmation---not a move to a harder simulator.
\end{abstract}

\section{Project goal, scope, and intended contribution}
The original research question is:
\begin{quote}
Can a cooperative, parameter-shared robot-soccer policy trained in a deliberately fast abstract
simulator transfer zero-shot, without weight updates, to an independent rigid-body simulator under
unseen dynamics, sensing, communication, and control perturbations; and does failure-directed
domain randomization transfer better than equal-budget nominal and uniform training?
\end{quote}

The intended tangible contribution is a controlled mechanism result, not a leaderboard: an
interpretable curriculum should spend experience on observed failure modes, improve a frozen
policy's cross-simulator robustness, preserve useful nominal behavior, and reveal which
perturbation families remain unsolved. The primary artifact is a reproducible 2-v-1 high-level
skill-selection study with independent physics implementations, fixed seeds, auditable run
records, paired evaluations, and representative videos.

Three boundaries prevent the scope from drifting. First, the seven macro-skills assume a lower
level locomotion and perception stack; this is not joint-level humanoid control. Second, Pymunk is
a sim-to-sim transfer target and only a proxy for selected sim-to-real questions. Third, the
current task can study coordination, but goal scoring alone cannot establish passing or rich team
play. Full 5-v-5/11-v-11 play, pixels, self-play populations, Webots, and physical deployment are
extensions only after the core transfer claim survives replication.

\subsection{Completion ladder}
The project has passed engineering validity and completed one full comparative pilot. It has not
yet passed training-seed replication, a delay-robustness gate, or a behaviorally grounded
cooperation gate. We will call the core project successful only if failure-directed MAPPO beats
both controls on a predeclared cross-physics profile metric across independent training seeds, is
not materially worse than nominal MAPPO on the delay--noise stress test, and preserves nominal
abstract competence. Passing/role behavior will be reported as a separate claim with explicit
opportunities and counts.

\section{Robot-soccer context and related work}
RoboCup's long-term objective is autonomous humanoid team play under the laws of football
\cite{kitano1997robocup}. That grand challenge is useful context, but it is not the scale of this
study. Work on simulated humanoid football uses a staged hierarchy from motor control, through
football drills, to long-horizon team play \cite{liu2022teamplay}; recent quadruped work similarly
combines learned low-level skills with high-level MAPPO and self-play before physical deployment
\cite{su2025quadruped}. Google Research Football also separates simpler academy scenarios from
full-game benchmarks \cite{kurach2020football}. These precedents support using a focused 2-v-1
scenario to isolate a transfer question rather than prematurely adding full-game complexity.

PPO provides the clipped on-policy objective \cite{schulman2017ppo}; MAPPO provides centralized
value learning with decentralized execution for cooperative tasks \cite{yu2022mappo}. Domain
randomization is a standard transfer technique \cite{tobin2017domain}, but uniform sampling can
produce suboptimal, high-variance policies, motivating adaptive sampling
\cite{mehta2020active}. Simulation-optimization bias is also a relevant threat: a policy may exploit
one simulator's model error rather than learn transferable behavior \cite{muratore2018domain}.
Finally, few-run deep-RL comparisons require explicit uncertainty and independent training runs
\cite{agarwal2021precipice}. These observations directly motivate the controls, cross-physics test,
and next replication gate used here.

\section{Task and method}
The task is a cooperative partially observable stochastic game with two attackers, one scripted
defender, and one shared team reward. Each attacker receives a distinct 61-dimensional egocentric
observation and independently samples from one shared categorical policy. The seven high-level
actions are approach, left/right dribble, shoot, pass, move to support, and hold/face ball.

Training occurs in an abstract simulator with explicit bounded acceleration, turn rate, ball drag,
contact and boundary handling, kick impulses, observation/action queues, and imperfect teammate
messages. Evaluation rebuilds players and ball as Pymunk rigid bodies using force/torque control,
collision shapes, substeps, and mass-scaled impulses. Observation, action, reward, and termination
interfaces are shared; actor weights and normalization are frozen during transfer.

MAPPO uses the shared decentralized actor and a centralized team-value critic. The failure-directed
curriculum evaluates 17 non-nominal profiles every 20 updates. If $f_i$ is the exponentially
smoothed failure rate for profile $i$, the conditional non-nominal sampling distribution is
\begin{align}
 w_i &= (\epsilon+f_i)^\alpha, \\
 p_i &= (1-\beta)\frac{w_i}{\sum_j w_j}+\frac{\beta}{N},
\end{align}
followed by capped-simplex projection. Training retains a 0.10 nominal-episode probability. The
uniform control samples the same enabled profile set and uses the same nominal probability and
training budget.

\section{Experimental protocol and audit standard}
Training, fixed abstract validation, curriculum evaluation, held-out abstract evaluation, Pymunk
transfer, and video seeds are separated. Every Phase 2 run used seed 0, commit
\texttt{45e80751}, an NVIDIA L4, 245 PPO updates, and
2,007,040 environment steps. Each frozen best actor has:
\begin{itemize}
\item 300 abstract standard episodes: 100 for each of stationary, chase-ball, and intercept;
\item 100 nominal Pymunk intercept transfer episodes;
\item 900 Pymunk profile episodes: 50 for each of 18 profiles including nominal; and
\item 500 Pymunk grid episodes: 20 in each action-delay by localization-noise cell.
\end{itemize}
Thus Phase 2 contains 5,400 training-method evaluation episodes, plus training validation and
videos. All three metadata records say \texttt{complete}; metrics are finite; no training or
evaluation exception is recorded. Approximate KL and clipping fractions stay small, and learning
rates decay nearly to zero, so simply extending the same jobs is not the right next action.

The confirmation protocol is now frozen in code. The exploratory 130k/140k evaluation ranges are
not reused: confirmatory abstract, transfer, profile, robustness, cooperation, and delay-audit
ranges begin at 230k, 240k, 241k, 242k, 250k, and 260k, respectively. An automated gate requires
three complete training seeds per method, FDR profile superiority over both controls with the same
direction in at least two matched seeds, grid non-inferiority within 0.05, and abstract-intercept
non-inferiority within 0.10.

\subsection{Training behavior}
Nominal MAPPO's rolling training success rises from 0.038 to 0.682 and its final fixed nominal
validation reaches 0.825. Uniform and failure-directed training are harder, ending at rolling
success 0.275 and 0.269; their final nominal validation is 0.450 and 0.475. The best observed
nominal validation for uniform and failure-directed training is 0.600 and 0.525. Entropy falls in
all three runs without numerical instability. This is expected evidence of a robustness--nominal
specialization trade-off, although a single training seed cannot establish its typical size.

The failure curriculum updates 12 times. Its final probability ranges only from 0.0359 to 0.0823
and entropy is 2.795, close to the 17-profile maximum $\log 17=2.833$. It is therefore adaptive but
not concentrated. The highest final weights include high action delay, low ball friction,
medium observation noise, and combined conditions. Curriculum measurements use only five episodes
per profile per update, so each raw success estimate moves in increments of 0.2 and is noisy.

\subsection{What transferred}
Failure-directed MAPPO obtains 0.640 success on the canonical nominal Pymunk transfer set, compared
with 0.440 for nominal and 0.380 for uniform MAPPO. It also raises the 18-profile mean to 0.521 from
0.417 and 0.416. On the 900 profile episodes it lowers out-of-bounds termination to 0.226
(nominal 0.354), invalid-action fraction to 0.157 (nominal 0.236; uniform 0.381), and redundant
chasing to 0.042 (nominal 0.117; uniform 0.100). It improves on nominal MAPPO in 12 of 18 individual
profiles, especially nominal Pymunk (0.680), fast movement (0.820), slow movement (0.620), low
observation noise (0.640), and communication profiles (0.580--0.640). This is a meaningful
single-checkpoint transfer result, not a trivial re-expression of training return.

It is not yet a learned-policy victory over the deliberate Pymunk heuristics from Phase 1. On the
same nominal transfer seeds, double-chase and role-based success is 0.680 and 0.660 versus FDR's
0.640; neither paired contrast is detectably different. FDR exceeds random's 0.490 by 0.150, but
the exact paired result is borderline ($p=0.053$). The present positive claim is therefore about
FDR relative to equal-budget learned MAPPO controls, not best-overall soccer performance.

\subsection{What did not transfer}
The delay--noise grid reverses the headline ordering: nominal MAPPO scores 0.494, failure-directed
0.366, and uniform 0.346. Failure-directed success at zero action delay is 0.65 averaged over noise
levels, then falls to 0.38, 0.25, 0.32, and 0.23 for delays one through four. Its named
\texttt{action\_delay\_high} and \texttt{action\_delay\_medium} results are only 0.260 and 0.240,
despite high delay receiving the curriculum's largest final sampling weight. Meanwhile nominal
MAPPO's grid average increases rather than decreases at several nonzero delays. One plausible
inference is that delay smooths an overly reactive nominal action sequence, but queue, reset, and
macro-repeat traces must be inspected before treating that as mechanism rather than implementation
artifact.

The adaptive curriculum therefore found hard profiles but did not solve its hardest family. Its
five-episode updates may chase noise; a feed-forward actor with three frames of history may also be
structurally weak under multi-step delayed control. The combined moderate/severe profile successes
of 0.380/0.320, below nominal's 0.580/0.540, reinforce this concern. These results rule out a broad
claim that failure-directed training is already the most robust method.

\subsection{Cooperation and videos}
Phase 2 includes six failure-directed MP4s: two abstract successes and one abstract out-of-bounds
failure, plus two Pymunk successes and one Pymunk out-of-bounds failure. They verify rendering,
outcome labels, approach/shot behavior, and useful spatial separation. They do not show a passing
strategy. Across 900 profile episodes, valid pass counts are only 5/12 for nominal, 29/71 for
uniform, and 4/6 for failure-directed MAPPO; canonical Pymunk transfer contains zero completed
passes for all methods. Consequently the reported completion proportions have tiny denominators.
The reduced redundant-chase fraction supports role separation, but learned passing cooperation is
not demonstrated.
\subsection{Reporting defect discovered and repaired by the audit}
The pilot's generic comparison exporter grouped only by method and simulator. It consequently
averaged the 100-episode transfer test, 900-profile suite, and 500-episode stress grid, and treated
differences between unlike suites as error bars. The underlying episode CSVs and trained policies
were not corrupted. The repaired exporter makes the exact evaluation name a grouping key, retains
the training seed, computes canonical gaps only from same-run abstract-intercept and Pymunk-transfer
rows, and writes a seed-level replication summary. A regression test now prevents unlike suites
from being pooled. Tables~\ref{tab:phase2-suite-results} and
\ref{tab:phase2-paired-results} retain suite identity.

\section{Decision: a yellow light, not a graduation}
The project is on its intended path. It has moved beyond trivial infrastructure: two independent
dynamics implementations, approximately six million Phase 2 training steps, 5,400 frozen-policy
evaluation episodes, an adaptive curriculum, and paired scenario-level differences reveal a real
and interpretable method effect. At the same time, the scientific objective is not complete. There
is one training seed, the two robustness suites disagree, the FDR actor is delay-fragile, and the
task has not elicited meaningful passing. We should proceed to a focused Phase 2 confirmation, but
we should not yet claim general superiority or move to Webots/full-game soccer.

\section{Implemented confirmation workflow and execution order}
The following sequence is implemented in the cleaned Phase 2 notebook. Expensive commands are
restart-safe and each completed or failed run is copied to Drive immediately. The notebook restores
the seed-0 checkpoints, but never extends their training; it runs seeds 1 and 2 only when a matching
complete run is absent. Notebook execution, rather than further design work, is now the next step.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{P0---audit delay semantics without retraining.} Run deterministic trace tests for
the queued command at latencies 0--5, queue state at reset, macro-action repeat, and action age.
Replay matched grid seeds for all three frozen actors and log chosen, queued, and executed actions.
Exit criterion: every latency has a hand-checkable expected trace, and the nominal improvement
with delay has a behavioral explanation or a fixed bug.

\item \textbf{P0---use the repaired evaluation/report semantics.} Keep suite as a required grouping key,
generate canonical transfer gaps only from abstract-intercept versus Pymunk-transfer rows, and
report completed/attempted passes rather than an unsupported percentage. Mark the current
130k/140k ranges as exploratory because they have now informed decisions; allocate untouched
confirmatory ranges. Exit criterion: an automated test prevents unlike suites from being pooled.

\item \textbf{P0---run the cooperation probe, not a new simulator.} Use the fixed mirrored 2-v-1
evaluation scenario in which the carrier's direct shooting lane is blocked and the support player
has the viable lane. Log pass opportunities, attempts, completions, receiver possession, and goals
after a pass. Record matched-seed videos for all three actors. This determines whether the current
reward/action design can support the word ``cooperative.''

\item \textbf{P1---run the smallest decisive replication.} After P0 freezes semantics, train
seeds 1 and 2 for nominal, uniform, and failure-directed MAPPO: six new jobs, identical budgets,
with no tuning on confirmatory evaluation seeds. Report every seed and a seed-aware interval. The
predeclared primary result is 18-profile Pymunk mean success; require FDR to exceed both controls.
Require grid AUC to be non-inferior to nominal within 0.05 and nominal abstract-intercept success
to be non-inferior within 0.10. If the direction is stable in at least two of three seeds, expand to
five seeds for the final claim; otherwise diagnose or report a negative result rather than sweep.

\item \textbf{P1---target the delay mechanism only if replication preserves the failure.} The
first ablation is FDR with the action-latency parameter neutralized, including inside combined
profiles. It is gated in the notebook and cannot run merely because the cell is executed. Compare
it with a more reliable curriculum estimate (more than five episodes per profile, or uncertainty-
aware weighting) and, if needed, a recurrent actor. Change one mechanism at a time. Communication
and observation-noise ablations are secondary because current evidence points most strongly to
delay.

\item \textbf{P2---produce the final story package.} Create matched same-seed videos and traces,
suite-specific figures, per-seed tables, confidence intervals, failure taxonomy, and a short
reproducible command manifest. The intended story is either a replicated targeted-transfer gain
with a known boundary, or an honest finding that adaptive failure sampling overfits noisy/hard
profiles. Both are useful; cherry-picked success clips are not.

\item \textbf{P3---increase fidelity only after the core gate passes.} A third simulator or
Webots becomes valuable once the same frozen actor robustly transfers across current physics and
the behavior probe shows a real cooperative skill. Physical deployment additionally requires
perception/localization, safe locomotion, kick control, watchdogs, and hardware logs; the successful
zero-shot biped work relied on high-frequency control and targeted dynamics randomization
\cite{haarnoja2023soccer}, capabilities outside the present stack.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Work to avoid now}
Do not extend the seed-0 runs after the learning rate has decayed, tune repeatedly against the now-
inspected test seeds, average unlike suites, treat episode-level $p$-values as training-run
replication, claim cooperation from redundant-chase alone, run all scaffolded ablations at once,
or divert effort to full-game/self-play/Webots before the delay contradiction is resolved.

\section{Limitations and claim boundaries}
The environment omits images, recurrence, learned opponents, joint-level locomotion, falls,
actuator and sensing limits, and physical safety. Macro-skill adapters encode soccer structure.
Pymunk can be easier than the abstract simulator for some trajectories, so a negative transfer gap
is not automatically evidence of realism or robustness. The paired tests condition on one actor
per method; only independent training seeds address optimizer variance. Finally, the curriculum's
profile definitions and this analysis were informed by current outcomes, so new confirmatory seeds
must remain untouched until the next protocol is frozen.

\section{Conclusion}
Phase 2 is complete as a pilot and has yielded a worthwhile result: failure-directed MAPPO is
substantially better on nominal Pymunk transfer and most named perturbation profiles, with cleaner
action and role metrics, but it is markedly worse than nominal MAPPO on delayed-control stress
tests. That tension is the research finding and the next target. The project remains aligned with
its original objective; it should now become narrower and more decisive, not broader. Resolve the
delay semantics, establish whether cooperation is real, and run six carefully controlled
replication jobs. Only a replicated profile gain with no material delay-grid regression justifies
graduating to a harder simulator.
