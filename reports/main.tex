\begin{abstract}
We present a compact multi-fidelity testbed for parameter-shared multi-agent reinforcement
learning in a two-attacker robot-soccer task. Policies train in a fast abstract simulator and are
frozen before zero-shot evaluation in an independently implemented Pymunk rigid-body simulator.
The proposed curriculum directs domain randomization toward perturbation profiles on which the
current policy fails. Pymunk transfer is sim-to-sim evaluation and a controlled proxy for selected
sim-to-real robustness questions, not physical deployment. Final performance claims are deferred
until the registered experiment artifacts are generated.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and motivation}
Robot soccer combines partial observability, delayed control, contact dynamics, communication
failure, and cooperation. End-to-end physical training is expensive, while conclusions drawn from
one simulator can be brittle. We therefore ask whether a decentralized, cooperative skill policy
learned in a deliberately simple simulator survives a change in simulator fidelity and unseen
perturbations. The contribution is a failure-directed curriculum that spends training episodes on
the policy's empirically weakest named perturbation profiles.

\section{RoboCup and robot-soccer context}
RoboCup established robot soccer as a long-horizon benchmark joining perception, control, and
multi-agent reasoning \cite{kitano1997robocup}. Modern learned soccer controllers demonstrate the
value of reinforcement learning for dynamic bipedal play \cite{haarnoja2023soccer}. Our scope is
narrower: high-level behavior selection for two abstract attackers, assuming lower-level motion
and perception stacks.

\section{Related work}
PPO uses a clipped surrogate objective to stabilize on-policy updates
\cite{schulman2017ppo}. MAPPO showed that careful centralized-value training can be effective in
cooperative multi-agent tasks \cite{yu2022mappo}; this repository uses centralized training with
decentralized execution. Domain randomization varies simulator parameters to improve transfer
\cite{tobin2017domain}, while automatic curricula adapt the distribution as learning proceeds
\cite{openai2019rubik}. The present method uses explicit, interpretable failure profiles rather
than an unstructured Cartesian parameter distribution. Cross-fidelity evaluation follows the
broader motivation of using simulation variation to reveal model-specific policy behavior.

\section{Problem formulation}
We model the task as a cooperative partially observable stochastic game
$\langle\mathcal{S},\{\mathcal{O}_i\},\{\mathcal{A}_i\},P,r,\gamma\rangle$.
Two agents receive distinct egocentric observations $o_i$, select discrete skills through one
shared policy $\pi_\theta(a_i\mid o_i)$, and receive one team reward. During MAPPO training only, a
critic $V_\phi(s)$ observes the true joint state. The actor remains decentralized at evaluation.

\section{Abstract simulator}
The training simulator explicitly integrates bounded acceleration, speed, turn rate, ball drag,
collisions, boundary and goal events, kick impulses, a delayed-action queue, perception queues, and
imperfect teammate messages. Each policy step repeats a selected macro-action for four low-level
steps. The scripted defender can guard, chase, or predict an interception point and clears the ball
through ordinary limited motion rather than teleportation.

\section{Pymunk transfer simulator}
The transfer environment rebuilds players, ball, walls, and goal openings as Pymunk rigid bodies.
Velocity targets are realized by bounded forces and torque; kicks are impulses delivered at
contact range. Inertia, collision response, damping, integration substeps, and reaction delay
differ unavoidably from the abstract equations. Observation, action, reward, and metric interfaces
remain fixed, so the TorchScript actor transfers unchanged. This is zero-shot cross-simulator
transfer, not completed physical sim-to-real deployment.

\section{Observation and action design}
Each 61-dimensional local vector contains self motion and action state, delayed ball history,
egocentric goalposts and boundaries, teammate kinematics and stale message state, defender state,
confidence flags, and identity. The seven actions are approach, left/right dribble, shoot, pass,
move to support, and hold/face ball. Noise affects perceived state only; true dynamics and
centralized state remain separate.

\section{IPPO and MAPPO}
Both algorithms use one categorical actor. IPPO shares a local value network and computes one
advantage per local trajectory. MAPPO uses one centralized team value per environment step and
broadcasts the team advantage to the two actor samples; its value loss is counted once. GAE
bootstraps through time-limit truncation but does not propagate across a reset. Separate Adam
optimizers, clipped policy and value objectives, entropy regularization, gradient clipping,
target-KL stopping, and linear learning-rate decay follow standard PPO practice.

\section{Failure-directed domain randomization}
Let $f_i$ be a smoothed failure rate for profile $i$. After warm-up, deterministic validation on
disjoint seeds updates
\begin{align}
 w_i &= (\epsilon + f_i)^\alpha,\\
 \tilde p_i &= (1-\beta)\frac{w_i}{\sum_j w_j}+\frac{\beta}{N}.
\end{align}
The distribution is projected onto the simplex with a hard maximum profile probability. The
uniform mixture preserves exploration; the cap prevents one persistent failure from consuming all
training. Curriculum evaluation never updates observation normalization.

\section{Experimental protocol}
Training, ordinary validation, curriculum validation, abstract test, Pymunk transfer, and video
seeds occupy disjoint ranges. Policies are selected on fixed abstract validation seeds, frozen,
then evaluated on nominal defenders, all profiles, and an action-delay by localization-noise grid.
Multiple training seeds are aggregated by experiment name. Checkpoints include all optimizer,
normalizer, RNG, schedule, and curriculum state needed to resume.

\section{Baselines and ablations}
The matrix includes random, double-chase, and hysteretic role-based heuristics; nominal IPPO;
nominal MAPPO; uniformly randomized MAPPO; and failure-directed MAPPO. Focused ablations remove
action-delay, communication, or observation-noise profile families while retaining the same code
path and configuration inheritance.

\section{Metrics}
Primary measures are goal success, return, time to score, and the abstract-to-Pymunk success gap.
Secondary measures include pass completion, possession, redundant chasing, collisions, invalid
skills, action switches, worst-decile return, CVaR, per-profile minima, normalized robustness area,
and bootstrap intervals. Reward-component logs distinguish sparse task reward from small
potential-based progress, possession, and support shaping.

\section{Qualitative behavior and failure analysis}
The video workflow records nominal, transfer, and forced-profile trajectories with action and
outcome overlays. Analysis should distinguish coordination failures (role oscillation, redundant
chasing, poor support), control failures (delay and movement response), perception/communication
failures, and contact-model failures. Representative successes and failures will be selected only
after the fixed-seed evaluation completes.

\section{Limitations and claim boundaries}
The task omits images, recurrent memory, joint-level locomotion, self-play, learned opponents, and
full-team tactics. The scripted skill adapters still encode useful soccer structure. Pymunk does
not model sensing, compliance, actuator limits, falls, or hardware damage. Consequently the study
is multi-fidelity and sim-to-real-oriented; it cannot establish physical transfer or safety.

\section{Conclusion}
The repository supplies an auditable experiment for asking whether adaptive, failure-directed
variation improves cooperative policy robustness across simulator implementations. Conclusions
about comparative performance remain pending the planned multi-seed artifact set. A later Webots
or robot phase should be attempted only after the simulator protocol is reproduced and a complete
perception, localization, locomotion, and safety stack is available.
